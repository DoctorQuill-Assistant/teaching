Marathon Lesson 0003: Long runs and how to pace them

The long run is one of the main pillars of marathon training. It helps build endurance, patience, confidence, and durability. But the long run only works well if you pace it with control.

Your objective for today

Understand what the long run is for, how it should usually feel, and how to avoid the common mistake of starting too fast.

Start patient. Finish in control.

What the long run is actually for

  • Build endurance so longer time on your feet feels more normal.
  • Build durability so your legs and connective tissues adapt gradually.
  • Build patience so race-day pacing feels more disciplined.
  • Build confidence from practicing steady, repeatable work.

The long run is not mainly for proving toughness. It is for teaching your body and mind to handle duration calmly.

How most long runs should feel

  • early: relaxed and almost a little too easy
  • middle: steady and controlled
  • late: still manageable, though naturally a bit more tiring

If the first third of your long run feels excitingly fast, you are probably going too hard.

The most common mistake

Many runners start long runs too quickly because they feel fresh, want a nice average pace, or get pulled along by good weather and good mood.

  • The first few kilometers feel easy.
  • Pace creeps up.
  • Effort rises quietly.
  • The final third becomes a grind.

A smart long run should get harder because it is long, not because you paced it badly.

Simple pacing rule for long runs

  1. First part: hold back on purpose.
  2. Middle part: stay smooth and conversational or close to it.
  3. Final part: let the effort rise only slightly if you still feel strong.

For most of your year, long-run discipline matters more than long-run speed.

How to know the pace is right

  • You are not fighting your breathing early.
  • You can keep your form calm and relaxed.
  • You are not worried about surviving the second half.
  • You finish tired but not wrecked.

When to slow down even more

  • warm weather
  • bad sleep
  • stressful week
  • rolling hills
  • building fatigue from recent training

On those days, protecting the effort matters more than protecting the pace number.

Mini scenario

You start your Sunday long run feeling fresh. After 20 minutes, your pace is clearly quicker than your normal easy pace, but it still feels fine for now.

  • Option A: keep it there because it still feels comfortable
  • Option B: ease back a little now so the second half stays under control

Best answer: Option B.

Good long runs reward restraint early.

Quiz 1: Fill in the blank

Quiz 2: Short self-check

  1. What is the main purpose of the long run?
  2. What is a common sign you started too fast?
  3. On a hot day, what matters more: matching normal pace or protecting effort?

Simple answer key

  • Main purpose: build endurance and durability.
  • Common sign: the last third becomes a grind because the early pace was too ambitious.
  • Protecting effort matters more.

Your takeaway

The best long runs are usually the ones where you stay calm early, steady through the middle, and finish feeling like you trained well rather than survived badly.
